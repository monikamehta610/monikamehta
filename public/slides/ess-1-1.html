Environmental Value Systems (EVS)

1.1.1
What Is a Perspective?

Key UnderstandingA perspective is how a particular situation is viewed and understood by an individual. It is based on a mix of personal and collective assumptions, values, and beliefs.

What Shapes a Perspective?

  • Personal experience: direct encounters with environmental issues
  • Cultural background: traditions, norms, and values of the community
  • Education: formal and informal learning about the environment
  • Social context: family, peers, media influence
  • Geographic location: urban vs rural, developed vs developing
Key point: No two individuals have exactly the same perspective, even when raised in the same environment. Perspectives are shaped by the unique intersection of all the factors above.

1.1.3
Values

Key UnderstandingValues are qualities or principles that people feel have worth and importance in life.

What Are Values?

  • Core beliefs about what matters most
  • Guide behaviour and decision-making
  • Developed through socialization and experience
  • Can be individual or shared by groups
  • Are relatively stable but can change over time

Examples of Environmental Values

  • Conservation: protecting nature for its own sake
  • Preservation: keeping wilderness untouched
  • Utilitarian: nature has value because it provides resources
  • Aesthetic: nature is beautiful and worth protecting
  • Economic: environment = financial asset
Values underpin perspectives — understanding someone's values helps you understand why they hold a particular perspective on environmental issues.

1.1.4
Values in Action

Key UnderstandingThe values that underpin our perspectives can be seen in our communication and actions with the wider community. The values held by organizations can be seen through advertisements, media, policies and actions.

Individual Values → Actions

  • Recycling behaviour reflects environmental values
  • Consumer choices (organic, fair trade) signal priorities
  • Protest and activism express deeply held values
  • Voting patterns reflect environmental priorities

Organizational Values → Communication

  • Advertisements: what a company promotes reveals its priorities
  • Media: how issues are framed reflects underlying values
  • Policies: environmental regulations reveal societal values
  • Corporate actions: CSR reports, sustainability goals

1.1.5
Investigating Perspectives with Values Surveys

Key UnderstandingValues surveys can be used to investigate the perspectives shown by a particular social group towards environmental issues.

How Values Surveys Work

  • Questionnaires or interviews designed to reveal underlying values
  • Can compare perspectives between different social groups
  • May use Likert scales, ranking exercises, or open-ended questions
  • Results help identify patterns in environmental attitudes

Example: Comparing Two Groups

A survey might ask: "How important is economic growth compared to protecting the environment?" Results could reveal that urban professionals value conservation more than rural farmers who depend on resource extraction.

In the exam, you may be asked to design or evaluate a values survey. Consider: sample size, representativeness, question design, and potential biases.

1.1.6
Environmental Worldviews

Key UnderstandingWorldviews are the lenses shared by groups of people through which they perceive, make sense of, and act within their environment. They shape people's values and perspectives through culture, philosophy, ideology, religion and politics.

What Is an Environmental Worldview?

An environmental worldview is a set of assumptions and beliefs about how the world works and humanity's relationship with nature. It acts as a lens through which all environmental information is interpreted.

Key distinction: A perspective is an individual's view; a worldview is a shared lens held by a group. Worldviews shape the values that underpin individual perspectives.

1.1.7
The Environmental Value System

Key UnderstandingAn environmental value system (EVS) is a model that shows the inputs affecting our perspectives and the outputs resulting from our perspectives.
EVS Model Inputs (culture, education, experience, media) → Perspective → Actions & Communication

Inputs → Perspective → Outputs

Inputs (shaping perspective):

  • Cultural traditions
  • Education system
  • Media coverage
  • Personal experiences
  • Religious beliefs
  • Scientific knowledge

Outputs (from perspective):

  • Behaviour and lifestyle choices
  • Consumer decisions
  • Voting patterns
  • Communication with others
  • Advocacy and activism
  • Career choices

1.1.9
How Perspectives Change

Key UnderstandingPerspectives and the beliefs that underpin them change over time in all societies. They can be influenced by government or NGO campaigns or through social and demographic change.

What Drives Change in Perspectives?

  • Government campaigns: policy shifts, public awareness programs
  • NGO campaigns: Greenpeace, WWF raising awareness
  • Social change: generational shifts (younger generations often more environmental)
  • Demographic change: urbanization, migration, education levels
  • Technology: access to information changes understanding
  • Media: documentaries (e.g., Blue Planet II → plastic awareness)
